Topic 01

Four-Probe Method

Why Four-Probe Over Two-Probe?

In the two-probe method, contact resistance and spreading resistance are large, so the true resistivity cannot be separated from the measured value.

The four-probe method solves this by separating current injection from voltage sensing:

  • Outer two probes — supply current from the source meter
  • Inner two probes — measure voltage; draw no current (high-impedance voltmeter)
  • Probe and contact resistance do not contribute to the sample measurement

Physical setup: Four equally spaced tungsten tips, spring-loaded, uniform spacing s. Two measurement techniques:

Four-point collinear probe Van der Pauw
Collinear Probe — Bulk Material
Condition: $t \gg s$

When thickness is much greater than probe spacing, current spreads spherically from each outer probe tip.

Derivation — Bulk Resistivity

Area of spherical shell at radius $x$ from probe tip:

$$A = 2\pi x^2$$
— hemisphere

Differential resistance element:

$$\Delta R = \frac{\rho \, dx}{A} = \frac{\rho \, dx}{2\pi x^2}$$

Integrate between inner probes (limits $s \to 2s$), accounting for superposition of both outer probes:

$$R = \int_s^{2s} \frac{\rho \, dx}{2\pi x^2} = \frac{\rho}{2\pi} \left[-\frac{1}{x}\right]_s^{2s} = \frac{\rho}{2\pi} \left(\frac{1}{s} - \frac{1}{2s}\right) = \frac{\rho}{4\pi s}$$

Superposition of two outer probes gives $R = V/(2I)$. Substituting:

$$\frac{V}{2I} = \frac{\rho}{4\pi s}$$

Rearranging for resistivity:

$$\rho = \frac{V}{I} \cdot 2\pi s$$
Collinear Probe — Thin Sheet
Condition: $t \ll s$

When thickness is much less than probe spacing, current spreads in cylindrical rings (2D geometry), not spheres.

Derivation — Thin Sheet Resistivity

Area of cylindrical ring at radius $x$, height $t$:

$$A = 2\pi x t$$

Differential resistance:

$$\Delta R = \frac{\rho \, dx}{2\pi x t}$$

Integrate from $s \to 2s$:

$$R = \int_s^{2s} \frac{\rho \, dx}{2\pi x t} = \frac{\rho}{2\pi t} [\ln x]_s^{2s} = \frac{\rho}{2\pi t} \ln 2$$

Using $R = V/(2I)$:

$$\frac{V}{2I} = \frac{\rho \ln 2}{2\pi t}$$

Rearranging:

$$\rho = \frac{Vt\pi}{I \ln 2}$$
Advantages Over Two-Probe
  • Contact and spreading resistances are negligible at voltage probes
  • High accuracy — probe resistance is not added to sample measurement
  • Suitable for measuring very low resistance values
  • Works on both bulk and thin-film specimens
Topic 02

Van der Pauw Method

Conditions — All Must Be Satisfied
  • Sample must have a flat shape of uniform thickness
  • Thickness must be much less than width and length
  • Sample must be homogeneous and isotropic
  • Sample must have no isolated holes
  • All four contacts must be located at the edges / periphery
  • Contact area must be at least 10× smaller than total sample area
  • Symmetrical geometry preferred — ensures $f_A = f_B = 1$

Preferred geometry: Cloverleaf  ·  Acceptable: square with corner contacts  ·  Not recommended: contacts inside perimeter

Contacts are numbered 1 → 4 clockwise from top-left.

Measurement Procedure

Eight voltage measurements are made around the periphery with different current injection configurations. Two resistivity values are then derived:

$\rho_A$ — Horizontal configuration
Equation A $$\rho_A = \frac{\pi}{\ln 2} \cdot f_A \cdot t_s \cdot \frac{V_1 - V_2 + V_3 - V_4}{4I}$$
$\rho_B$ — Vertical configuration
Equation B $$\rho_B = \frac{\pi}{\ln 2} \cdot f_B \cdot t_s \cdot \frac{V_5 - V_6 + V_7 - V_8}{4I}$$
Variables $t_s$ — sample thickness (cm)
$f_A, f_B$ — geometrical correction factors (= 1 for perfect symmetry)
$I$ — current through sample (A)
$V_1$ – $V_8$ — voltages measured by voltmeter
Average Resistivity
$$\rho_{\text{AVG}} = \frac{\rho_A + \rho_B}{2}$$
Properties Measurable by This Method
  1. Resistivity
  2. Doping type — p-type or n-type
  3. Density of majority carriers (per unit area)
  4. Charge density and doping level
  5. Mobility of majority carriers
Topic 03

Hall Effect

Definition

When a current-carrying conductor (metal or semiconductor) is placed in a transverse magnetic field, an electric field is produced inside the conductor in a direction normal to both the current and the magnetic field. This is the Hall Effect. The resulting voltage is the Hall voltage $V_H$.

Geometry of the Setup
  • Conventional current along +OX
  • Magnetic field $B$ along +OY
  • Hall field $E_H$ develops along OZ
Case I — N-type Semiconductor or Metal
N-type / Metal
  • Majority carriers = electrons
  • Electrons move along −OX (opposite to conventional current)
  • Magnetic force on electron: $F = -Bev$ → acts along +OZ
  • Electrons accumulate at face ABEF → ABEF becomes negative, OCDG becomes positive
  • Hall field $E_H$ builds up, opposing further accumulation until equilibrium
Derivation — Hall Coefficient (N-type)

At equilibrium, electric force balances magnetic force:

$$-eE_H = -Bev \quad \Rightarrow \quad E_H = Bv \quad \cdots(1)$$

Current density $J$ relates to carrier velocity:

$$J = -nev \quad \cdots(2)$$
$$v = \frac{J}{-ne} \quad \cdots(3)$$

Substitute (3) into (1):

$$E_H = \frac{BJ}{-ne} \quad \cdots(4)$$

Hall coefficient is defined as $R_H = E_H / BJ$. Substituting (4):

$$R_H = \frac{E_H}{BJ} = \frac{1}{-ne}$$
$$R_H = -\frac{1}{ne} \quad \text{(negative for N-type)}$$
Case II — P-type Semiconductor
P-type Semiconductor
  • Majority carriers = holes (positively charged)
  • Holes move along +OX — same direction as conventional current
  • Magnetic force: $F = +Bev$ → acts along +OZ
  • Holes accumulate at face ABEF → ABEF becomes positive, OCDG negative
  • Hall voltage $V_H$ has opposite sign compared to N-type → used to identify carrier type
$$R_H = +\frac{1}{pe} \quad \text{(positive for P-type)}$$
Experimental Determination of $R_H$

A rectangular slab: thickness $t$, width $w$, current $I$, magnetic field $B \perp I$.

Derivation — $R_H$ from Measurable Quantities

Hall field from measured voltage:

$$E_H = \frac{V_H}{w} \quad \cdots(8)$$

Current density from geometry:

$$J = \frac{I}{wt} \quad \cdots(11)$$

Substitute into $R_H = E_H / BJ$:

$$R_H = \frac{V_H / w}{B \cdot I / wt} = \frac{V_H \cdot t}{I \cdot B}$$

Final expression:

$$R_H = \frac{V_H \cdot t}{I \cdot B} \quad \text{[m}^3\text{/coulomb]}$$
Applications
1. Determine Semiconductor Type

$R_H$ negative → N-type  |  $R_H$ positive → P-type

2. Carrier Concentration
$$n = \frac{1}{e \cdot R_H} \quad \text{or} \quad p = \frac{1}{e \cdot R_H}$$
3. Carrier Mobility
$$\mu_e = \frac{\sigma_n}{ne} = -\sigma_n \cdot R_H$$ $$\mu_h = \frac{\sigma_p}{pe} = \sigma_p \cdot R_H$$
4. Magnetic Flux Density
$$B = \frac{V_H \cdot t}{R_H \cdot I}$$
Topic 04

C-V Measurement

Background

Introduced by Hillibrand and Gold (1960) to determine majority carrier concentration in semiconductors.

C-V measurements yield quantitative information about:

  • Diffusion potential and doping concentration
  • Doping profile as a function of depth from the junction

Applicable to:

PN junctions Schottky barriers Electrolyte-semiconductor MIS field effect
Principle

The capacitance at a PN junction depends on the charge-depletion layer formed at the junction. The depletion region is swept free of mobile carriers by the built-in drift field, leaving behind ionized donors and acceptors — behaving like a parallel-plate capacitor:

$$C = \frac{\varepsilon A}{W}$$

where $W$ = depletion width, $A$ = junction area, $\varepsilon$ = permittivity.

Varying the applied voltage changes $W$, which changes $C$. Plotting $C$ vs $V$ gives the doping profile as a function of depth.

PN Junction — Poisson's Equation

In the depletion region, Poisson's equation governs the electrostatics:

Poisson's Equation $$\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial E}{\partial x} = \frac{\rho(x)}{\varepsilon} = \frac{e}{\varepsilon} \left[p(x) - n(x) + N_D^+(x) - N_A^-(x)\right]$$
For p-side  ($-x_p \leq x < 0$)
$$-\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x^2} \approx \frac{e}{\varepsilon} \cdot N_A^-$$
For n-side  ($0 < x \leq x_n$)
$$-\frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial x^2} \approx \frac{e}{\varepsilon} \cdot N_D^+$$

Solving these three equations yields the potential distribution:

$$V(x) = V_{bi} \left[ 2\left(\frac{x}{w}\right) - \left(\frac{x}{w}\right)^2 \right]$$
Built-in Potential
$$V_{bi} = \frac{KT}{e} \cdot \ln\left(\frac{N_d \cdot N_a}{n_i^2}\right)$$

where $N_d$, $N_a$ = donor/acceptor concentrations  |  $n_i$ = intrinsic carrier concentration  |  $KT/e \approx 0.026$ V at 300 K

Four Key Diagrams — Abrupt PN Junction (No Bias)

Depletion approximation. Dashed lines show majority carrier tails at the edges of the depletion region.
BElectric Field
Triangular profile across depletion region. Peaks at junction ($x = 0$), zero at both depletion edges.
